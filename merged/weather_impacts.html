What about severe weather changes?

Hurricanes

Has the number or intensity of hurricanes changed?


About our hurricane models

The data used to analyze hurricane frequency and intensity ranged back to the 1850s, and therefore did not include the Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale categorization scheme we have become accustomed to seeing (Category 1, 2, etc.). Therefore, a k-means clustering model was used to classify the hurricanes based on their minimum pressure and mazimum wind speed. The model was optimized to group the huricanes into 4 categories.

Next, linear regression models were used to project the total number of hurricanes we could potentially see up until the year 2200, as well as the number of hurricanes in each category. The models used inputs such as historical changes in sea temperature anomaly, sea level, and global temperature to make these forecasts. As can be seen by this chart, the frequency as well as intensity of hurricanes is projected to increase over time based on current historical data.

Tornadoes

Has the number or intensity of tornadoes changed?


About our tornado models

Linear regression models were used to project the number of tornadoes we could see until the year 2200, in addition to the number of magnitude 0 (F0 on the Fujita scale) and magnitude 2 (F2 on the Fujita scale) tornadoes. These 2 magnitudes were chosen for display as they demonstrated relatively reliable linear regression accuracies of above 70%. This is likely due to the fact that there is a notable increase in tornadoes of these magnitudes seen in the available historical data. The models used inputs such as historical changes in cardon dioxide emissions, gas fuel emissions, and global temperature to make these forecasts.

It is worth mentioning that the model shows the number of projected magnitude 0 tornadoes is greater than the total tornadoes projected. As these were created from separate linear regression models, the intent is to display the future trends that could be seen, rather than predict the exact frequency that can be expected.
