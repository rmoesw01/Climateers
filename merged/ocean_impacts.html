at the north pole

Shrinkage of Northern Sea Ice


Sea ice at the North Pole is rapidly dwindling.

GeoTIFFs from NSIDC, Sea Ice Index, Sept. 1980-2020

The average extent of northern sea ice has changed dramatically since 1980 as seen in the corresponding figure. Hover over the sea ice (depicted in white) to view the numerical measure of the area. The interactive globe can also be dragged and zoomed out.

This is consistent with rising global temperatures, both atmospheric and oceanic. This also directly contributes to rising sea levels, which in turn leads to flooding and higher likelihood of hurricane formation.

GeoTIFF files of September sea ice extent every 5 years were obtained from the NSIDC, polygonized with QGIS, and further manipulated with mapshaper to return files in geoJSON format.

rising tides

Sea Level & Sea Temperature


The seas are warmer, and sea levels are rising.

data source here?

As global temperatures steadily rise and the average extent of northern sea ice depletes, the sea level continues to increase each year. To model this increase, a linear regression model was utilized with inputs including historical global temperature, sea temperature, and carbon dioxide emissions.

A comparison was done between the machine learning linear regression model and the linear regression performed on only the historical sea level data. As can be seen, these two methodologies output very similar forecasts, with the machine learning model projecting slightly lower sea levels by the year 2200.

In addition to sea level analysis, a linear regression model was also employed to predict sea temperature change. This model applied historical changes in sea level, global temperature, and glacier mass to forecast sea temperature change (anomaly) up to the year 2200.

In both sea temperature and sea level models, a steady increase is predicted.
